East Africa Energy Indicators

Period: 2017–2022

Executive Summary

Between 2017 and 2022, East Africa has shown consistent growth across key energy indicators. Access to electricity, electric power consumption, energy use per capita, and renewable energy consumption all exhibit positive annual slopes, indicating steady expansion. Projections for 2023 foresee access to electricity reaching approximately 58.0%, electric power consumption at about 115.9 kWh per capita, energy use per capita near 437.3 kg of oil equivalent, and renewable energy consumption around 85.8%. Correlations with GDP remain close to zero or slightly negative for most metrics, suggesting that energy growth is not strongly tied to GDP movements, with renewable consumption showing a modest positive relationship.

Insights

Access to electricity is growing at over 3% per year but remains below 60% coverage, highlighting ongoing electrification challenges. Electric power consumption per capita mirrors this trend with modest annual increases, reflecting gradual infrastructure improvements. Energy use per capita is expanding more slowly, suggesting gains in efficiency or a relatively low baseline usage. Renewable energy consumption, while increasing at a slower rate than other metrics, constitutes a large share of the energy mix and shows the only positive correlation with GDP.

Anomalies and Trends

  • Energy use per capita is projected to rise substantially to 437.3 kg of oil equivalent by 2023, the highest absolute forecast among indicators.
  • Correlations between most energy metrics and GDP are slightly negative, indicating decoupling of energy growth from economic performance.
  • Renewables maintain the largest share of consumption (85.8%) despite the lowest annual growth slope, underlining reliance on traditional bioenergy.
  • Access to electricity growth outpaces consumption growth, suggesting successful grid expansion efforts even in lower-demand regions.

Recommendations

  1. Accelerate grid and off-grid electrification programs to push coverage above 60% and reduce energy poverty.
  2. Invest in modern renewable technologies (solar, wind) to diversify beyond traditional biomass and strengthen positive GDP linkages.
  3. Promote energy efficiency initiatives to balance growth in per-capita energy use with sustainability goals.
  4. Align national energy strategies with economic planning to re-couple energy metrics with GDP growth.
East Africa Country-Level Growth & Energy Correlations

East Africa Country-Level Growth & Energy Correlations

Period: 2017–2022

Executive Summary

Between 2017 and 2022, Ethiopia led East Africa with average annual GDP growth of 6.96%, followed by Rwanda (6.25%), Tanzania (4.82%), Uganda (4.49%), and Kenya (4.46%). In every country, GDP per capita growth correlates almost perfectly with overall growth (r≈0.99). Country-specific energy and economic linkages vary markedly, revealing both growth drivers and potential bottlenecks.

Insights

Ethiopia: Strong growth drivers include GDP per capita growth and revenue mobilization, while rapid infrastructure rollout has created short-term inflationary pressures and lagged gains in grid access and energy efficiency.

Rwanda: Expansion is supported by imported energy and higher power use, but hydropower variability and low renewable output temper the clean-energy contribution.

Tanzania: Growth is powered by private-sector energy investments and high-tech manufacturing, even as traditional renewables and alternative energy shares decline in relative importance.

Uganda: Oil export booms and efficiency improvements drive cycles, whereas rising renewable consumption and reserve accumulation coincide with slower growth phases.

Kenya: Dependence on energy imports and higher consumption underpins growth, while renewable generation assets have yet to yield positive economic linkages.

Anomalies & Trends

  • Ethiopia’s negative correlation with grid access suggests that rapid expansion areas may not immediately contribute to GDP growth.
  • Uganda’s strong inverse link to renewables highlights potential over-reliance on biomass and hydropower in growth periods.
  • Tanzania’s pivot away from renewables toward gas and PPP projects is evident in declining renewable output correlations.
  • Kenya’s thermal-heavy mix explains the sharp negative ties to renewable output and consumption.

Recommendations

  1. Ethiopia: Integrate electrification with targeted economic zones to convert access into growth and manage inflation.
  2. Rwanda: Diversify energy imports with domestic renewable capacity to stabilize output and growth linkages.
  3. Tanzania: Rebalance energy policy to reinvigorate renewable generation alongside gas-based expansion.
  4. Uganda: Leverage renewable resource potential to smooth growth cycles and reduce dependency on oil exports.
  5. Kenya: Accelerate deployment of geothermal and wind projects to align energy investments with economic gains.
